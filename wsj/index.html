Wonderous Statistical Journeys

Hey, check out this graph:

Great news for the Mexican blueberry industry, happy for it.

But more importantly this was the first chart I made for a professional newsroom. It accompanied “Mexico’s Berry Bounty Fuels U.S. Trade Dispute” by Robbie Whelan, nearly seven years ago for The Wall Street Journal.

Everytime I look at it, indeed, it makes me laugh. Getting back to Adobe Illustrator after a few years of primarily code-based work. The unreasonable pride in getting the dopey alliterative headline approved by copy. That the WSJ Markets Blue™ perfectly evoked blueberries.

A thrilling, monumental chart!

I’m sure you agree.

After falling into a rabbit hole of old hard drives, I came across that chart, and other pieces from my time at WSJ.

Presented here, some favorites.

1.

Sneaking Colleague's Cats Into Graphics

There is no subject on earth you can more readily get dozens of reference photos for than cats. Stand tall, announce “send me pictures of your cat”, and co-workers will leap, spring-loaded to answer the call.

Cat graphics came up a couple of times, and each was an opportunity seized.

3.

Maps

By and away the most frequent maps I made for WSJ were quick-hit breaking-news locator maps. “Here’s where this thing happened” or “here’s a 3 step-journey of [news event]”.

My favorites were the exception to that, where the story warranted a more indulgent basemap, or allowed for a more experimental style.

Above is a satellite and relief composite of Japan made for “Fukushima, ten year’s later”. Japan’s dynamic landscape informs why so many cities were along the coastline, and the satellite image further helped convey the natural areas against the dense cityscapes.

All that is secondary to the data itself, so in the eventual piece it was dilluted considerably. That’s the right choice. But I’m still quite chuffed by how dang pretty Japan is alone.

Most news isn’t good news. Even when it is, instilling character into a visual piece can be fraught. Every so often you get

4.

Mistakes into Marmalade

Here’s Moby Dick in it’s entirity,

Back in 2019, myself and Joel Eastwood were tasked with figuring out a visual for the upcomign Mueller Report. We knew it would be redacted, and that those redactions would be color coded. The second teh report dropped, we wanted to be the first to be able to say what % of the report was redacted for the given reasons.

In order to do that, we came turned to command line tools. Step one, merge all the pages into a single image. Then, calculate out how many pixels are black vs. the ones that are color coded. It’s a flawed math, but it seemed like a quick way to get the ballpark.

Unfortunately, when the report dropped the redactions looked like this instead:

Fortunately, when we tested out the process on Moby Dick, we had clocked that seeing 100+ pages altogether is a great visual. Within minutes of the report becoming available, we had the image tweeted out, and the next morning it would be the front page lead visual for the paper:

7.

Graphics Signing Off

The last visual I did in the office proper never saw publication. At some point the U.S. Graphics team had commandeered a white board. When I moved back to Wisconsin midway thru 2020 during the heart of the pandemic, I stopped in to collect a few items. On that whiteboard, did a little “farewell, for now” sketch.

If I recall correctly they found it a few months after the fact!

Overall my time at the Journal felt incredibly well spent. A heckuva crash course in data viz design, working alongside brilliant veterans and hotshots.

I have a few physical souviners from this time, a WSJ branded hat and a black WSJ coffee mug where the logo wiped off years ago. Those don’t quite feel reflective of the real work done there though. But you know what would?
